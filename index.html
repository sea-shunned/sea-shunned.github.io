Biography

I have recently completed my PhD, and am currently looking for opportunities.

My current research looks at clustering, particularly within the context of evolutionary algorithms. My latest work, HAWKS, is an EA for generating synthetic data that challenges and uncovers the (inductive) biases of clustering algorithms, helping to tackle the algorithm selection problem for clustering. It is designed to be (relatively) easy to use, and I’m always happy to hear how it’s been used.

My previous background is in biochemical engineering, which I still remain very much interested in. As a result, I hope to combine my more recent experience in machine learning with the healthcare domain, should the opportunity fortuitously arise.

This is a bare-bones website just to summarize my experience to-date. I aim to add further research content (though my previous publications are available). Please contact me for collaboration, questions, etc.

Publications

Cameron Shand, Richard Allmendinger, Julia Handl, Andrew Webb, John Keane
July 2019 GECCO 2019

Evolving Controllably Difficult Datasets for Clustering

Synthetic datasets play an important role in evaluating clustering algorithms, as they can help shed light on consistent biases, strengths, and weaknesses of particular techniques, thereby supporting sound conclusions. Here, we introduce HAWKS, a new data generator that uses an evolutionary algorithm to evolve cluster structure of a synthetic data set. We demonstrate how such an approach can be used to produce datasets of a pre-specified difficulty, to trade off different aspects of problem difficulty, and how these interventions directly translate into changes in the clustering performance of established algorithms.

Cameron Shand, Richard Allmendinger, Julia Handl, John Keane
July 2018 GECCO 2018

Towards an Adaptive Encoding for Evolutionary Data Clustering

Existing encodings for evolutionary clustering strike different trade-offs between model and search complexity, limiting the applicability to data sets with particular properties or to problems of moderate size. We use the hypervolume to identify convergence, triggering a change in the search space, comparing different strategies to focus the optimization in this new space. The most successful strategies meet the clustering performance achieved for an optimal (a priori) setting of the hyperparameter, at a ~40% reduction of computational expense.
